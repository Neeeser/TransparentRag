# Aurora Station Operations Handbook

Aurora Station is a fictional orbital research platform used as sample
content for retrieval testing. The document describes its power, thermal,
and crew-rotation systems in plain factual prose.

## Power generation

Aurora Station generates power from four deployable solar arrays, each
rated at 84 kilowatts at beginning of life. The arrays track the sun on
two-axis gimbals and feed a 900-volt primary bus. During orbital eclipse,
which lasts up to 36 minutes per 92-minute orbit, the station draws from
three lithium-iron-phosphate battery banks with a combined usable capacity
of 260 kilowatt-hours. Battery state of charge is never allowed below 30
percent; load-shedding begins automatically at 40 percent, starting with
the materials-science furnaces.

A fifth, non-deployable array on the zenith truss serves as a cold spare.
It is electrically isolated unless two primary arrays are offline, a
condition that has occurred once, during the debris-avoidance maneuver of
mission year three.

## Thermal control

Waste heat is collected by a single-phase ammonia loop running at 2.1
kilograms per second and rejected through two radiator wings with a
combined area of 110 square meters. Each radiator panel can be isolated by
motorized valves within eight seconds of a detected micrometeoroid puncture.
The loop's accumulator holds 14 liters of reserve ammonia, sized for two
full panel isolations without a resupply.

Internal cabin temperature is held between 21 and 24 degrees Celsius.
Avionics cold plates run a separate water loop that exchanges heat with the
ammonia loop through two redundant interface heat exchangers; a third
exchanger was removed in the block-two redesign to save 46 kilograms.

## Crew rotation

The station hosts six crew on overlapping 180-day rotations, staggered so
that no more than three crew members are ever in their first 30 days
aboard. Handover between rotations takes 12 days and includes a joint
emergency drill covering depressurization, ammonia leak, and fire in the
galley module. Crew exercise allocation is 2.5 hours per day on the
resistive device and treadmill combined, scheduled around the two daily
downlink windows.

## Communications

Primary communication uses a Ka-band phased array with a data rate of 600
megabits per second through relay satellites, with an S-band omni system as
backup at 2 megabits per second. Command uplink is dual-authenticated, and
a 90-second command loss timer places the station in free-drift safe mode
if both links drop during a maneuver.
